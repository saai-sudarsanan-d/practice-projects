<p><i><b>The Waste Land</b></i> is a poem by <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;A&#93;</a></sup> widely regarded as one of the most important poems of the 20th century and a central work of <a href="/wiki/Modernist_poetry_in_English" title="Modernist poetry in English">modernist poetry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Favourite_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Favourite-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Published in 1922, the 434-line<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;B&#93;</a></sup> poem first appeared in the United Kingdom in the October issue of Eliot's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Criterion" title="The Criterion">The Criterion</a></i> and in the United States in the November issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dial" title="The Dial">The Dial</a></i>. It was published in book form in December 1922. Among its famous phrases are "April is the cruellest month", "I will show you fear in a handful of dust", and the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">mantra</a> "<a href="/wiki/Shanti_Mantras" title="Shanti Mantras">Shantih shantih shantih</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;C&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eliot's poem combines the legend of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fisher_King" title="Fisher King">Fisher King</a> with vignettes of contemporary British society. Eliot employs many literary and cultural allusions from the <a href="/wiki/Western_canon" title="Western canon">Western canon</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a>. The poem shifts between voices of <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a> featuring abrupt and unannounced changes of <a href="/wiki/Narrator" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrator">speaker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">location, and time</a> and conjuring a vast and dissonant range of cultures and literatures.
</p><p>The poem is divided into five sections. The first, "The Burial of the Dead", introduces the diverse themes of disillusionment and despair. The second, "A Game of Chess", employs alternating narrations, in which vignettes of several characters address those themes experientially. "The Fire Sermon", the third section, offers a philosophical meditation in relation to the imagery of death and views of self-denial in juxtaposition, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_religions" title="Eastern religions">Eastern religions</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2023)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After a fourth section, "Death by Water", which includes a brief lyrical petition, the culminating fifth section, "What the Thunder Said", concludes with an image of judgment.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_history">Composition history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:168px;"><a href="/wiki/File:T.S._Eliot,_1923.JPG" class="image"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG/166px-T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG" decoding="async" width="166" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG/249px-T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG/331px-T.S._Eliot%2C_1923.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1289" data-file-height="1580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:T.S._Eliot,_1923.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eliot in 1923</div></div></div>
<p>Eliot probably worked on the text that became <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Waste_Land" class="extiw" title="s:The Waste Land"><i>The Waste Land</i></a> for several years preceding its first publication in 1922. In a May 1921 letter to New York lawyer and patron of modernism <a href="/wiki/John_Quinn_(collector)" title="John Quinn (collector)">John Quinn</a>, Eliot wrote that he had "a long poem in mind and partly on paper which I am wishful to finish".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1988451_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1988451-7">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Aldington" title="Richard Aldington">Richard Aldington</a>, in his memoirs, relates that "a year or so" before Eliot read him the manuscript draft of <i>The Waste Land</i> in London, Eliot visited him in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAldington1941261_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAldington1941261-8">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> While walking through a graveyard, they discussed <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gray" title="Thomas Gray">Thomas Gray</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Elegy_Written_in_a_Country_Churchyard" title="Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard">Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard</a></i>. Aldington writes: "I was surprised to find that Eliot admired something so popular, and then went on to say that if a contemporary poet, conscious of his limitations as Gray evidently was, would concentrate all his gifts on one such poem he might achieve a similar success."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAldington1941261_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAldington1941261-8">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eliot, having been diagnosed with some form of <a href="/wiki/Nervous_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Nervous disorder">nervous disorder</a>, had been recommended rest, and applied for three months' leave from the bank where he was employed; the reason stated on his staff card was "<a href="/wiki/Mental_breakdown" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental breakdown">nervous breakdown</a>". He and his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Vivienne_Haigh-Wood_Eliot" title="Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot">Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot</a>, travelled to the coastal resort of <a href="/wiki/Margate,_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Margate, Kent">Margate, Kent</a>, for a period of convalescence.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> While there, Eliot worked on the poem, and possibly showed an early version to <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> when the Eliots travelled to Paris in November 1921 and stayed with him. Eliot was en route to <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, for treatment by Doctor Roger Vittoz, who had been recommended to him by <a href="/wiki/Ottoline_Morrell" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoline Morrell">Ottoline Morrell</a>; Vivienne was to stay at a <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanatorium</a> just outside Paris. In Hotel Ste. Luce (where Hotel Elite has stood since 1938) in Lausanne, Eliot produced a 19-page version of the poem.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1971xxii_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1971xxii-10">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He returned from Lausanne in early January 1922. Pound then made detailed editorial comments and significant cuts to the manuscript. Eliot later dedicated the poem to Pound. Letters written by both Vivienne and Eliot give some insight into the creation of the poem.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscript_drafts">Manuscript drafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manuscript drafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eliot sent the manuscript drafts of the poem to John Quinn in October 1922; they reached Quinn in New York in January 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;D&#93;</a></sup> Upon Quinn's death in 1924 they were inherited by his sister Julia Anderson. Years later, in the early 1950s, Mrs Anderson's daughter Mary Conroy found the documents in storage. In 1958 she sold them privately to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a>.
</p><p>It was not until April 1968, three years after Eliot's death, that the existence and whereabouts of the manuscript drafts were made known to <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Eliot" title="Valerie Eliot">Valerie Eliot</a>, the poet's second wife and widow.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1971xxix_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1971xxix-13">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1971, <a href="/wiki/Faber_and_Faber" title="Faber and Faber">Faber and Faber</a> published a "facsimile and transcript" of the original drafts, edited and annotated by Valerie Eliot. The full poem prior to the Pound editorial changes is contained in the facsimile.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editing">Editing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Editing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:136px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg" class="image"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg/134px-Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg" decoding="async" width="134" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg/201px-Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg/267px-Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg 2x" data-file-width="804" data-file-height="1160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ezra_Pound_by_EO_Hoppe_1920.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ezra Pound, a major editor of the work</div></div></div>
<p>The drafts of the poem reveal that it originally contained almost twice as much material as the final published version. The significant cuts are in part due to Ezra Pound's suggested changes, although Eliot himself also removed large sections.
</p><p>The opening lines of the poem—"April is the cruellest month, breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land"—did not originally appear until the top of the second page of the typescript. The first page of the typescript contained 54 lines in the sort of street voice that we hear again at the end of the second section, <i>A Game of Chess</i>. This page appears to have been lightly crossed out in pencil by Eliot himself.
</p><p>Although there are several signs of similar adjustments made by Eliot, and a number of significant comments by Vivienne, the most significant editorial input is clearly that of Pound, who recommended many cuts to the poem.
</p><p>'The typist home at teatime' section was originally in entirely regular stanzas of <a href="/wiki/Iambic_pentameter" title="Iambic pentameter">iambic pentameter</a>, with a rhyme scheme of <i>abab</i>—the same form as Gray's <i>Elegy</i>, which was in Eliot's thoughts around this time. Pound's note against this section of the draft is "verse not interesting enough as verse to warrant so much of it". In the end, the regularity of the four-line stanzas was abandoned.
</p><p>At the beginning of 'The Fire Sermon' in one version, there was a lengthy section in <a href="/wiki/Heroic_couplet" title="Heroic couplet">heroic couplets</a>, in imitation of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">The Rape of the Lock</a></i>. It described one lady Fresca (who appeared in the earlier poem "Gerontion"). <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ellmann" title="Richard Ellmann">Richard Ellmann</a> said "Instead of making her toilet like Pope's Belinda, Fresca is going to it, like <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">Joyce</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Bloom" title="Leopold Bloom">Bloom</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The lines read:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>Leaving the bubbling beverage to cool,<br />
Fresca slips softly to the needful stool,<br />
Where the pathetic tale of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Richardson" title="Samuel Richardson">Richardson</a><br />
Eases her labour till the deed is done ...
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>Ellmann notes: "Pound warned Eliot that since Pope had done the couplets better, and Joyce the defecation, there was no point in another round."
</p><p>Pound also excised some shorter poems that Eliot wanted to insert between the five sections. One of these, that Eliot had entitled 'Dirge', begins
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>Full fathom five your Bleistein lies<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;E&#93;</a></sup><br />
Under the flatfish and the squids.<br />
<br />
<a href="/wiki/Graves%27_disease" title="Graves&#39; disease">Graves' disease</a> in a dead Jew's eyes!<br />
Where the crabs have eat the lids<br />
...
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>At the request of Eliot's wife Vivienne, three lines in the <i>A Game of Chess</i> section were removed from the poem: "And we shall play a game of chess / The <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> men make company between us / Pressing lidless eyes and waiting for a knock upon the door". This section is apparently based on their marital life, and she may have felt these lines too revealing. However, the "ivory men" line may have meant something to Eliot: in 1960, thirteen years after Vivienne's death, he inserted the line in a copy made for sale to aid the <a href="/wiki/London_Library" title="London Library">London Library</a>, of which he was president at the time; it fetched £2,800.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Hart-Davis" title="Rupert Hart-Davis">Rupert Hart-Davis</a> had requested the original manuscript for the auction, but Eliot had lost it long ago (though it was found in America years later).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a late December 1921 letter to Eliot to celebrate the "birth" of the poem, Pound wrote a bawdy poem of 48 lines entitled "Sage Homme" in which he identified Eliot as the mother of the poem but compared himself to the midwife.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1988498_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1988498-18">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The first lines are:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>These are the poems of Eliot<br />
By the <a href="/wiki/Uranian_poetry" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranian poetry">Uranian</a> Muse begot;<br />
A Man their Mother was,<br />
A Muse their Sire.<br />
How did the printed Infancies result<br />
From Nuptials thus doubly difficult?<br />
If you must needs enquire<br />
Know diligent Reader<br />
That on each Occasion<br />
Ezra performed the Caesarean Operation.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publishing_history">Publishing history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publishing history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the editing had even begun, Eliot found a publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;F&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Horace_Liveright" title="Horace Liveright">Horace Liveright</a> of the New York publishing firm of Boni &amp; Liveright was in Paris for a number of meetings with Ezra Pound. At a dinner on 3 January 1922 (see <a href="/wiki/1922_in_poetry" title="1922 in poetry">1922 in poetry</a>), he made offers for works by Pound, <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i>) and Eliot. Eliot was to get a royalty of 15% for a book version of the poem planned for autumn publication.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To maximise his income and reach a broader audience, Eliot also sought a deal with magazines. Being the London correspondent for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dial" title="The Dial">The Dial</a></i> magazine<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and a college friend of its co-owner and co-editor, <a href="/wiki/Scofield_Thayer" title="Scofield Thayer">Scofield Thayer</a>, <i>The Dial</i> was an ideal choice. Even though <i>The Dial</i> offered $150 (£34)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> for the poem (25% more than its standard rate) Eliot was offended that a year's work would be valued so low, especially since another contributor was found to have been given exceptional compensation for a short story.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The deal with <i>The Dial</i> almost fell through (other magazines considered were the <i>Little Review</i> and <i>Vanity Fair</i>), but with Pound's efforts eventually a deal was worked out where, in addition to the $150, Eliot would be awarded <i>The Dial</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s second annual prize for outstanding service to letters. The prize carried an award of $2,000 (£450).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In New York in the late summer (with John Quinn, a lawyer and literary patron, representing Eliot's interests) Boni &amp; Liveright made an agreement with <i>The Dial</i> allowing the magazine to be the first to publish the poem in the US if they agreed to purchase 350 copies of the book at discount from Boni &amp; Liveright.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Boni &amp; Liveright would use the publicity of the award of <i>The Dial'</i>s prize to Eliot to increase their initial sales.
</p><p>The poem was first published in the UK, without the author's notes, in the first issue (October 1922) of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Criterion" title="The Criterion">The Criterion</a></i>, a literary magazine started and edited by Eliot. The first appearance of the poem in the US was in the November 1922 issue of <i>The Dial</i> magazine (actually published in late October). In December 1922, the poem was published in the US in book form by Boni &amp; Liveright, the first publication to print the notes. In September 1923, the <a href="/wiki/Hogarth_Press" title="Hogarth Press">Hogarth Press</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Private_press" title="Private press">private press</a> run by Eliot's friends <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Woolf" title="Leonard Woolf">Leonard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>, published the first UK book edition of <i>The Waste Land</i> in an edition of about 450 copies, the type handset by Virginia Woolf.
</p><p>The publication history of <i>The Waste Land</i> (as well as other pieces of Eliot's poetry and prose) has been documented by Donald Gallup.<sup id="cite_ref-Gallup_1969_pp._29–31,_208_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gallup_1969_pp._29–31,_208-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eliot, whose 1922 annual salary at <a href="/wiki/Lloyds_Bank" title="Lloyds Bank">Lloyds Bank</a> was £500 ($2,215),<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> made approximately £630 ($2,800) with <i>The Dial</i>, Boni &amp; Liveright, and Hogarth Press publications.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;G&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eliot originally considered entitling the poem <i>He do the Police in Different Voices</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot19714_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot19714-30">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the version of the poem Eliot brought back from Switzerland, the first two sections of the poem—'The Burial of the Dead' and 'A Game of Chess'—appeared under this title. This strange phrase is taken from <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>' novel <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Mutual_Friend" title="Our Mutual Friend">Our Mutual Friend</a></i>, in which the widow Betty Higden says of her adopted foundling son Sloppy, "You mightn't think it, but Sloppy is a beautiful reader of a newspaper. He do the Police in different voices." Some critics use this working title to support the theory that, while there are many different voices (speakers) in the poem, there is only one central consciousness. What was lost by the rejection of this title Eliot might have felt compelled to restore by commenting on the commonalities of his characters in his note about <a href="/wiki/Tiresias" title="Tiresias">Tiresias</a>, stating that 'What Tiresias sees, in fact, is the substance of the poem.'
</p><p>In the end, the title Eliot chose was <i>The Waste Land</i>. In his first note to the poem he attributes the title to <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Weston_(scholar)" title="Jessie Weston (scholar)">Jessie Weston</a>'s book on the <a href="/wiki/Grail" class="mw-redirect" title="Grail">Grail</a> legend, <i><a href="/wiki/From_Ritual_to_Romance" title="From Ritual to Romance">From Ritual to Romance</a></i>. The allusion is to the wounding of the <a href="/wiki/Fisher_King" title="Fisher King">Fisher King</a> and the subsequent sterility of his lands; to restore the King and make his lands fertile again, the Grail questor must ask, "What ails you?" In 1913, <a href="/wiki/Madison_Cawein" title="Madison Cawein">Madison Cawein</a> published a poem called "Waste Land"; scholars have identified the poem as an inspiration to Eliot.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The poem's title is often mistakenly given as "Waste Land" (as used by Weston) or "Wasteland", omitting the <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite article</a>. However, in a letter to Ezra Pound, Eliot politely insisted that the title was three words beginning with "The".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1988567_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1988567-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TheWasteLandEpigraph.jpg" class="image"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/80/TheWasteLandEpigraph.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="112" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TheWasteLandEpigraph.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The epigraph and dedication to <i>The Waste Land</i> showing some of the languages that Eliot used in the poem: Latin, Greek, English and Italian.</div></div></div>
<p>The poem is preceded by a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Epigraph_(literature)" title="Epigraph (literature)">epigraph</a> from chapter 48 of <i>The <a href="/wiki/Satyricon" title="Satyricon">Satyricon</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Petronius" title="Petronius">Petronius</a>:
</p>
<table>
<tbody><tr>
<td style="border-style: none solid none none; border-width: 1px;">Nam Sibyllam quidem Cumis ego ipse oculis meis vidi in ampulla pendere, et cum illi pueri dicerent: "Σίβυλλα τί Θέλεις"; respondebat illa: "άποΘανεῖν Θέλω."
</td>
<td>For once I myself saw with my own eyes the <a href="/wiki/Cumaean_Sibyl" title="Cumaean Sibyl">Sibyl</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cumae" title="Cumae">Cumae</a> hanging in a cage, and when the boys said, "Sibyl, what do you want?" she replied "I want to die."
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Following the epigraph is a dedication (added in a 1925 republication) that reads "For Ezra Pound: <i>il miglior fabbro</i>". Here Eliot is both quoting line 117 of Canto XXVI of <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Purgatorio" title="Purgatorio">Purgatorio</a></i>, the second cantica of the <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>, where Dante defines the troubadour <a href="/wiki/Arnaut_Daniel" title="Arnaut Daniel">Arnaut Daniel</a> as "the best smith of the mother tongue", and also Pound's title of chapter 2 of his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_Romance" title="The Spirit of Romance">The Spirit of Romance</a></i> (1910) where he translated the phrase as "the better craftsman".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPound200533_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPound200533-33">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This dedication was originally written in ink by Eliot in the 1922 Boni &amp; Liveright edition of the poem presented to Pound; it was subsequently included in future editions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilhelm1990309_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilhelm1990309-34">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The five parts of <i>The Waste Land</i> are entitled:
</p>
<ol><li>The Burial of the Dead</li>
<li>A Game of Chess</li>
<li>The Fire Sermon</li>
<li>Death by Water</li>
<li>What the Thunder Said</li></ol>

<p>The text of the poem is followed by several pages of notes, purporting to explain his metaphors, references, and allusions. Some of these notes are helpful in interpreting the poem, but some are arguably even more puzzling, and many of the most opaque passages are left unannotated. The notes were added after Eliot's publisher requested something longer to justify printing <i>The Waste Land</i> in a separate book.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;H&#93;</a></sup> Thirty years after publishing the poem with these notes, Eliot expressed his regret at "having sent so many enquirers off on a wild goose chase after Tarot cards and the Holy Grail".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is some question as to whether Eliot originally intended <i>The Waste Land</i> to be a collection of individual poems (additional poems were supplied to Pound for his comments on including them) or to be considered one poem with five sections.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The structure of the poem is also meant to loosely follow the vegetation myth and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> folklore surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Fisher_King" title="Fisher King">Fisher King</a> story as outlined by Jessie Weston in her book <i>From Ritual to Romance</i> (1920). Weston's book was so central to the structure of the poem that it was the first text that Eliot cited in his "Notes on the Waste Land".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The style of the poem is marked by the many allusions and quotations from other texts (classic and obscure; "<a href="/wiki/Highbrow" title="Highbrow">highbrow</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Low_culture" title="Low culture">lowbrow</a>") that Eliot peppered throughout the poem. In addition to the many "highbrow" references and quotes from poets like <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Baudelaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretti</a>, Eliot also included several references to "lowbrow" genres. A good example of this is Eliot's quote from the 1912 <a href="/wiki/Popular_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular song">popular song</a> "The Shakespearian Rag" by lyricists Herman Ruby and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Buck" title="Gene Buck">Gene Buck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> There were also a number of lowbrow references in the opening section of Eliot's original manuscript (when the poem was entitled <i>He Do The Police in Different Voices</i>), but they were removed from the final draft after Eliot cut this original opening section.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1971_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1971-39">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The style of the work in part grows out of Eliot's interest in exploring the possibilities of <a href="/wiki/Dramatic_monologue" title="Dramatic monologue">dramatic monologue</a>. This interest dates back at least as far as "<a href="/wiki/The_Love_Song_of_J._Alfred_Prufrock" title="The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock">The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock</a>". <i>The Waste Land</i> is not a single monologue like "Prufrock". Instead, it is made up of a wide variety of voices (sometimes in monologue, dialogue, or with more than two characters speaking).
</p><p><i>The Waste Land</i> is notable for its seemingly disjointed structure, indicative of the <a href="/wiki/Modernist_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernist literature">Modernist</a> style of <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i> (which Eliot cited as an influence and which he read the same year that he was writing <i>The Waste Land</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the Modernist style, Eliot jumps from one voice or image to another without clearly delineating these shifts for the reader. He also includes phrases from multiple foreign languages (Latin, Greek, Italian, German, French and Sanskrit), indicative of Pound's influence.
</p><p>In 1936, <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a> wrote about <i>The Waste Land</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForster194091–92_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForster194091–92-41">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Let me go straight to the heart of the matter, fling my poor little hand on the table, and say what I think <i>The Waste Land</i> is about. It is about the fertilizing waters that arrived too late. It is a poem of horror. The earth is barren, the sea salt, the fertilizing thunderstorm broke too late. And the horror is so intense that the poet has an inhibition and is unable to state it openly.
</p><div style="margin-left: 2em;" class="poem">
<p>What are the roots that clutch, what branches grow<br />
Out of this stony rubbish&#160;? Son of man,<br />
You cannot say, or guess, for you know only<br />
A heap of broken images.
</p>
</div>
<p>He cannot say '<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/avaunt" class="extiw" title="wikt:avaunt">Avaunt!</a>' to the horror, or he would crumble into dust. Consequently, there are outworks and blind alleys all over the poem—obstacles which are due to the nature of the central emotion, and are not to be charged to the reader. <i>The Waste Land</i> is Mr. Eliot's greatest achievement. It intensifies the drawing-room premonitions of the earlier poems, and it is the key to what is puzzling in the prose. But, if I have its hang, it has nothing to do with the English tradition in literature, or law or order, nor, except incidentally, has the rest of his work anything to do with them either. It is just a personal comment on the universe, as individual and as isolated as <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Shelley</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Unbound_(Shelley)" title="Prometheus Unbound (Shelley)">Prometheus</a></i>.
</p><p>
... <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Manley_Hopkins" title="Gerard Manley Hopkins">Gerard Manly Hopkins</a> is a case in point—a poet as difficult as Mr. Eliot, and far more specialized ecclesiastically, yet however twisted his diction and pietistic his emotion, there is always a hint to the layman to come in if he can, and participate. Mr. Eliot does not want us in. He feels we shall increase the barrenness. To say he is wrong would be rash, and to pity him would be the height of impertinence, but it does seem proper to emphasize the real as opposed to the apparent difficulty of his work. He is difficult because he has seen something terrible, and (underestimating, I think, the general decency of his audience) has declined to say so plainly.</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sources from which Eliot quotes, or to which he alludes, include the works of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petronius" title="Petronius">Petronius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeidmann200998–108_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeidmann200998–108-42">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Saint <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Edmund Spenser</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Nerval" title="Gérard de Nerval">Gérard de Nerval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kyd" title="Thomas Kyd">Thomas Kyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">Thomas Middleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Webster" title="John Webster">John Webster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Marvell" title="Andrew Marvell">Andrew Marvell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Charles Baudelaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Goldsmith" title="Oliver Goldsmith">Oliver Goldsmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Hesse" title="Hermann Hesse">Hermann Hesse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Paul Verlaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>.
</p><p>Eliot also makes extensive use of Scriptural writings including the Bible, the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a></i>, the Hindu <i><a href="/wiki/Brihadaranyaka_Upanishad" title="Brihadaranyaka Upanishad">Brihadaranyaka Upanishad</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" class="mw-redirect" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Sermon" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire Sermon">Fire Sermon</a></i>, and of cultural and <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropological</a> studies such as Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Frazer" class="mw-redirect" title="James Frazer">James Frazer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough" title="The Golden Bough">The Golden Bough</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Weston_(scholar)" title="Jessie Weston (scholar)">Jessie Weston</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/From_Ritual_to_Romance" title="From Ritual to Romance">From Ritual to Romance</a></i> (particularly its study of the <a href="/wiki/Wasteland_(mythology)" title="Wasteland (mythology)">Wasteland</a> motif in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic mythology</a>).
</p><p>Eliot wrote in the original head note that "Not only the title, but the plan and a good deal of the incidental symbolism of the poem were suggested by Miss Jessie L Weston".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;I&#93;</a></sup> The symbols Eliot employs, in addition to the Waste Land, include the <a href="/wiki/Fisher_King" title="Fisher King">Fisher King</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">Tarot</a> Deck, the <a href="/wiki/Chapel_perilous" title="Chapel perilous">Chapel perilous</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Grail" class="mw-redirect" title="Grail">Grail</a> Quest.
</p><p>According to Valerie Eliot, the character Marie in "The Burial of the Dead" is based on <a href="/wiki/Countess_Marie_Larisch_von_Moennich" title="Countess Marie Larisch von Moennich">Marie Larisch</a>, whom Eliot met at an indeterminate time and place.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliot1971126_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliot1971126-44">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies">Parodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parodies of this poem have also sprung up, including one by Eliot's contemporary <a href="/wiki/H._P._Lovecraft" title="H. P. Lovecraft">H. P. Lovecraft</a>, a poem provocatively titled "Waste Paper: A Poem of Profound Insignificance".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Written in 1923, it is regarded by scholars like <a href="/wiki/S._T._Joshi" title="S. T. Joshi">S. T. Joshi</a> as one of his best satires.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Cope" title="Wendy Cope">Wendy Cope</a> published a parody of <i>The Waste Land</i>, condensing the poem into five limericks, <i>Waste Land Limericks</i>, in her 1986 collection <i>Making Cocoa for <a href="/wiki/Kingsley_Amis" title="Kingsley Amis">Kingsley Amis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waste_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthony_Lane" title="Anthony Lane">Anthony Lane</a> writes that <i>The Waste Land</i> is "a symphony of shocks, and like other masterworks of early modernism, it refuses to die down... The shocks have triggered aftershocks, and readers of Eliot are trapped in the quake. Escape is useless." Eliot inspired many of his contemporaries to incorporate the mythic method, the use of classic sources in a modern setting.  <a href="/wiki/F._Scott_Fitzgerald" title="F. Scott Fitzgerald">F. Scott Fitzgerald</a> was heavily influenced by <i>The Waste Land</i> when writing <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Gatsby" title="The Great Gatsby">The Great Gatsby</a></i>. Fitzgerald's novel contains a "valley of ashes" which is a kind of waste land. <i>Gatsby</i> was also set in 1922, the year <i>The Waste Land</i> was published.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Fitzgerald compares Gatsby to <a href="/wiki/Trimalchio" title="Trimalchio">Trimalchio</a> (a working title was <i>Trimalchio in West Egg</i>) who is also referenced in <i>The Waste Land.</i> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_Also_Rises" title="The Sun Also Rises">The Sun Also Rises</a></i> features a Fisher King character in Jake Barnes. <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/As_I_Lay_Dying" title="As I Lay Dying">As I Lay Dying</a></i> was influenced by "The Burial of the Dead". In <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon" title="Thomas Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Gravity%27s_Rainbow" title="Gravity&#39;s Rainbow">Gravity's Rainbow</a></i>, the sound of the V-2 rocket is referred to as "what the thunder said." Cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Rowson" title="Martin Rowson">Martin Rowson</a> wrote and illustrated a graphic novel pastiche of <i>The Waste Land</i>, in which detective Chris Marlowe encounters many of the figures in the poem.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The title of <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Handful_of_Dust" title="A Handful of Dust">A Handful of Dust</a></i> comes from the poem, and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">Neil Gaiman</a> would use the phrase in his graphic novel series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sandman_(comic_book)" title="The Sandman (comic book)">The Sandman</a>.</i> In <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Coppola's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Now" title="Apocalypse Now">Apocalypse Now</a>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Brando</a>'s Kurtz has copies of <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Weston_(scholar)" title="Jessie Weston (scholar)">Weston</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/From_Ritual_to_Romance" title="From Ritual to Romance">From Ritual to Romance</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/James_George_Frazer" title="James George Frazer">Frazer's</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough" title="The Golden Bough"><i>The Golden Bough</i></a><i>,</i> two of Eliot's sources for the poem; he also quotes Eliot's "<a href="/wiki/The_Hollow_Men" title="The Hollow Men">The Hollow Men</a>". Lane writes that "the more closely you map <i>The Waste Land</i>, the more it assumes the shape of an isthmus; so much of the past, both public and personal, streamed into its making, and so much has flowed from it ever since. When one of its most resonant quatrains is declaimed through a megaphone by Anthony Blanche, the resident dandy of <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a></i>, he is obviously signaling the fashionable status of the poem, as its fame increased through the nineteen-twenties and thirties, but there's more to it than that. He is restoring, as it were, the adamantine beauty of the rhyming lines—pentameters in parenthesis, which embed the travails of the present day inside the remoteness of myth:  </p><blockquote><p>(And I Tiresias have foresuffered all 
</p><p>Enacted on the same divan or bed;  
</p><p>I who have sat by Thebes below the wall 
</p><p>
And walked among the lowest of the dead.)"<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>
